The Baltimore classification has remained unchanged since 1971 and has been adopted by the ICTV. While it is a human-designed taxonomy, it is constrained by the combinatorics of nucleic acid chemistry: the possible permutations of single/double strand, DNA/RNA, sense/antisense, and reverse transcription yield exactly 7 functional classes.

To understand why 7 groups and not more: the binary choices are (1) DNA vs RNA = phi=2 options, (2) single vs double strand = phi=2 options, (3) positive vs negative sense (RNA only) = phi=2 options, and (4) reverse transcription present/absent. The combinatorial expansion is constrained by chemical compatibility -- not all combinations are viable. Double-stranded RNA cannot have a "sense" distinction (eliminating 2 combinations), single-stranded DNA lacks sense-dependent translation (eliminating 1), and reverse transcription adds exactly phi=2 classes (one DNA-based, one RNA-based). The result: sigma-sopfr=7 viable genome strategies, a number that emerges from the intersection of n=6 arithmetic with nucleic acid chemistry.

The subdivision of Baltimore groups into DNA-based (I, II, VII) = n/phi = 3 and RNA-based (III, IV, V, VI) = tau = 4 is not arbitrary. It reflects the fundamental asymmetry between DNA and RNA in biology: DNA serves as stable storage (fewer replication strategies needed), while RNA's instability and direct translatability create more diverse replication pathways.
